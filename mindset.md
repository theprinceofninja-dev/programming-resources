# Mindset & How to Think

## Why Learn Programming
1. Critical thinking and problem solving — interpret problems, try solutions, build mental models
2. Opportunities — build your own product, combine with business knowledge
3. Automate repetitive tasks
4. Language of the future
5. Strong asset on your resume
6. Can be combined with any field

## How to Think Like a Programmer
- Describe to a machine *exactly* how to do something — be precise and clear
- Think step by step; machines don't make assumptions
- Break every problem into smaller parts
- Solve it in your head first, then on paper, then in code
- Write out the algorithm in plain language before writing any code
- The algorithm matters more than the syntax

## How Not to Fail
1. Don't distract yourself with plans and roadmaps — that's avoidance
2. Be patient and honest with yourself
3. Ask for help, communicate, and put in the work
4. Learn something that solves a real problem — be hireable
5. Don't confuse tools with roles (Django != backend, AWS != DevOps)
6. Build and participate in a community

## Skills You Can't Learn from Tutorials
These require experience, practice, and struggling with the problem before solving it:
1. **Domain knowledge** — real-world expertise in the area you're building for
2. **Programming habits** — subconscious, automatic skills built through repetition
3. **Software tools & efficiency** — IDEs, libraries, version control, CLI, frameworks
4. **Debugging and obscure problem solving** — patience and a systematic approach

## Problem Solving — Step by Step
1. Define the problem clearly
2. Identify root causes
3. Search for multiple solutions
4. Find the best-fit solution
5. Plan and execute
6. Measure how well the solution worked

## Critical Thinking Framework
- Structure: Data → Logic → Data
- Consider all possibilities; eliminate those that don't fit
- Maintain logical consistency throughout
- Apply rules consistently
- Classify the problem — it helps recall similar solutions
- Build an algorithm you can reuse for similar problems
- Break the problem into parts

## Interview — Why You Get Rejected
1. Being late
2. Nervousness and lack of confidence → fix by practicing mock interviews beforehand
3. Wrong technical answers
4. Being too rigid and overly formal
5. Showing unwillingness to work extra hours or juggle multiple tasks
6. Mentioning strengths/weaknesses that are unrelated to the role
7. Not knowing the company, its field, products, or mission
8. Speaking negatively about your current or past manager/company — they always want positive people
9. Not updating your CV before the interview — gives the impression you're disorganized

## UX — User Experience Principles (from project feedback)
When building any web or app project, always consider:
1. Show feedback messages after every action — success or failure (e.g. "Product added successfully")
2. Show a confirmation before irreversible actions like delete (e.g. "Are you sure you want to delete?")
3. When editing, pre-fill the form with existing data — don't show a blank form
4. When a form has an error, keep the user's entered data — don't clear everything
5. When navigating to another screen for an action, show context from the previous screen (e.g. show the category name when adding a service to it)
6. Have someone unfamiliar with your project test it — they'll notice things you never will

## Work & Communication Habits
- Everything that touches the customer directly must be the highest priority — it reflects your performance from their perspective
- Don't leave small but visible issues unfinished — they define how your work looks
- Communicate early when you hit problems — it saves everyone's time
- Take initiative; go beyond the minimum
- Care about design and aesthetics even if there's no designer on the team
- Build good working relationships with your team — make collaboration comfortable

## The AI Age — What Still Matters
- The amount of information you know matters less now — information is freely accessible
- **Your thinking process is what determines your results**
- Problem solving and critical thinking are the irreplaceable skills
- Engineering = optimization of resources to meet requirements
- Frameworks are just tools — if you can't solve problems with them, ChatGPT can beat you
- Technologies change fast — flexibility and adaptability are more valuable than memorized facts
- Exercises, tasks, and problems you can actually solve matter far more than how many courses you've watched
- Information is abundant — what's needed now is the **courage to apply**
- Don't track your progress obsessively — it drains energy and slows you down
- Always see yourself as a beginner — walk the right path, don't measure the distance
- Learn by doing — understand the purpose behind every concept when you encounter it

## Productivity — Finishing a Month of Work in a Day
Key principles:
1. Tie your work to a clear, specific goal
2. Schedule the day beforehand — set the target in advance
3. Make decisions the day before (food, clothes, tasks)
4. Wrap up loose ends the day before — texts, emails, calls
5. Organize your workspace ahead of time
6. Pause all life maintenance — outsource or do it later
7. Turn off all distractions — phone, app blockers
8. Work in focused flow blocks: 3 hours on, 1 hour non-stimulating recovery
9. Wake up and go directly into your highest priority task
10. Treat each focus block like a battle — it needs a trigger ritual (close the door, put on headphones, sip coffee)
11. Keep the day special and different from every other day

## Engineer vs Researcher
- Engineer: solve the problem by any means — companies fund engineers more for this
- Researcher: interested in knowledge even if the problem isn't solved — needs passion and purpose
- At 30: technical expertise + guide the team, discuss solutions, handle micro-tasks
- At 35-40+: move toward team leadership, direction, big picture — away from technical implementation
- Work 3 years in code, get your hands dirty — then you can guide others

## Progressive Project Development (Beginner Approach)
A great way to teach and build incrementally — each step adds real complexity:
1. Static HTML + CSS page — displays text and images
2. Add a backend that serves the text content; JavaScript fetches and renders it
3. Move content into a database; backend retrieves from there
4. Add an HTML form to edit/update the content and submit it
5. Add inline editing directly on the page — content becomes editable in place

This approach teaches the full stack naturally, layer by layer, without overwhelming beginners.

- Focus on foundational skills first; specialization follows naturally

## Code Review Feedback — Notes for Trainees
Lessons from real code review sessions (applicable universally):
1. When you have a question, search for the answer in the documentation/spec before asking
2. Before implementing a change or suggestion, discuss it first — rewriting code twice wastes everyone's time
3. Keep refactoring notes in mind while writing code the first time, not after
4. Variable names must always be descriptive and self-documenting
5. After finishing the code, take a short break, then re-read it from the perspective of an outside observer — you'll spot things to improve or simplify

## Teaching Math to a Weak Student — Methodology Notes
Key insights from tutoring sessions (applicable to teaching any technical subject):
- **Memorization basics first**: multiplication table, known squares, common roots — these must be automatic
- **Mental arithmetic shortcuts**: complement to nearest 10, fast multiplication, pair-with-pair reduction
- **Visualization**: imagine the problem — dimensions, distances, speeds, volumes, quantities — before calculating
- **Feedback loops**: verify work using the inverse operation; derive answers by eliminating wrong possibilities
- **Context retention**: keep the full problem context in mind (in writing or mentally) while solving parts of it
- **Pattern recognition**: understand when a pattern applies and when it doesn't
- **Order and calm**: organize your work, don't rush — messiness causes careless errors
- **Understand relationships**: know what things are, what they do, and how they connect to each other
- **Repetition builds habit**: practice until procedures become automatic
- **Understand equations and balance**: whatever you do to one side, do to the other
